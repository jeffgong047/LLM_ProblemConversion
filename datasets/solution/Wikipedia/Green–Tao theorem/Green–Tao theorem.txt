Overview of the proof[edit]
Green and Tao's proof has three main components:

SzemerÃ©di's theorem, which asserts that subsets of the integers with positive upper density have arbitrarily long arithmetic progressions. It does not a priori apply to the primes because the primes have density zero in the integers.
A transference principle that extends SzemerÃ©di's theorem to subsets of the integers which are pseudorandom in a suitable sense. Such a result is now called a relative SzemerÃ©di theorem.
A pseudorandom subset of the integers containing the primes as a dense subset. To construct this set, Green and Tao used ideas from Goldston, Pintz, and YÄ±ldÄ±rÄ±m's work on prime gaps.[5] Once the pseudorandomness of the set is established, the transference principle may be applied, completing the proof.
Numerous simplifications to the argument in the original paper[1] have been found. Conlon, Fox & Zhao (2014) provide a modern exposition of the proof.
